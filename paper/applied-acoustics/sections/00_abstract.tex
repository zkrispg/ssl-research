Hand-crafted microphone-array geometry priors are widely injected into deep
sound event localization and detection (SELD) systems, often on the assumption
that explicit spatial layout can only help. We test this assumption with a
controlled one-bit intervention in the official DCASE~2024 SELD baseline: a
geometry bias is toggled on or off inside an otherwise identical
channel-attention block. Across two input modalities (MIC and FOA), three
temporal backbones (CRNN+MHSA, Conformer, Transformer), and five paired seeds
on real STARSS23 recordings, the prior's effect on directional accuracy is not
universal but graded by temporal inductive bias. It benefits the recurrent FOA
model ($-5.0\degsym$ class-dependent localization error, $\dz=-1.03$), remains
near-neutral to weakly helpful in the Conformer, and harms the pure-attention
Transformer (up to $+5.7\degsym$, $\dz=+0.71$). A factorial test over the
recurrent and Transformer extremes confirms an architecture$\times$prior
interaction ($F=4.62$, $p=0.039$; seed-matched paired contrast $p=0.029$), and
the recurrent FOA benefit replicates zero-shot on STARSS22 ($\dz=-1.50$,
$p=0.029$). A linear probe finds no significant change in linearly decodable
direction, indicating a behavioural rather than simple representational shift.
Finally, a second injection mechanism that adds geometry as an early
convolutional feature bias does not provide a clean cross-mechanism replication:
its contrasts move across reruns, checkpoint-selection paths, and modalities,
thereby bounding rather than strengthening the primary GCA claim. We conclude
that explicit geometry priors are architecture-structured, injection-sensitive
design choices rather than universal SELD improvements.
